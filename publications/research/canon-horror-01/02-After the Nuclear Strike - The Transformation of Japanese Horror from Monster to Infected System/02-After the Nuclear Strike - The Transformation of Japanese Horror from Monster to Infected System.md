## Introduction: Why “Strike” Rather than “Disaster”

In this article I use the term **“after the nuclear strike”** rather than the neutral word “disaster.” This is not a stylistic preference but a methodological position: the notion of a “strike” preserves the external causation of violence and prevents the historical subject of action from dissolving into impersonal disaster rhetoric [@dower1995a]. This framing clarifies why Japanese cultural memory in the second half of the twentieth century repeatedly returns to motifs of the damaged body, invisible agency, contagion, and long-term social stigma [@napier1993a].

The core thesis unfolds in three sequential layers: **prewar cultural ground → external historical strike of 1945 → internal national nightmare of the 1990s**.[^3] This sequence matters because it reveals not a sudden emergence of horror, but a historical recalibration of an already existing cultural environment. In other words, 1945 did not create Japanese horror culture from scratch; it radically transformed its language, optics, and anthropological focus [@dower1995a; @napier1993a]. For contrast with the spatial logic of the Ukrainian corpus, see Companion working paper (Canon Horror Series, 03, 2026): *When Disaster Becomes Environment: Chernobyl and the Spatial Model of Horror*.

At the same time, it is essential to emphasize that 1945 is not the beginning of history, but a radical rupture within it. Japan enters this moment not only as a victim of the nuclear strike, but also as a participant in imperial war and an ally of the Axis powers, which places the strike inside a broader context of total twentieth-century conflict. This does not negate the specificity of the trauma that followed; rather, it situates the external “strike” within a wider logic of war. Postwar cultural processing of horror is therefore formed within a tension between memory of one’s own destruction and the need to reinterpret prior experience of violence.

## 1. Prewar Cultural Ground: Modernization and Readiness for the Language of Extremity

The prewar stage should be understood as part of the argument itself, rather than as an external prelude. This is justified: by the mid-twentieth century, Japan was already in intensive dialogue with European literature and philosophy, actively reworking questions of subjectivity, suffering, inner conflict, and extreme psychic states [@dower1995a].

For analysis, this implies the following. The aesthetics of “after the nuclear strike” and mutation did not fall onto empty ground but onto an already developed intellectual terrain. Therefore, later images of deformed bodies, blurred borders between human and nonhuman, and persistent motifs of guilt and survival should be read not only as reactions to 1945, but also as products of long cultural modernization [@dower1995a].

Methodologically, this thesis protects against the simplification that “everything is explained by the bomb.” On the contrary, the bomb acts as an **accelerator** and **radicalizer** of already existing cultural tensions, transferring them into a new representational regime—one in which trauma is simultaneously bodily, medial, and political [@dower1995a; @napier1993a].

## 2. The Historical Rupture of 1945: The Body as the Primary Archive of Violence

In the primary-source block (Hiroshima/Nagasaki archives, memorial platforms, survivor testimony), what matters most is that memory of 1945 is fixed through the language of the body: burns, “black rain,” delayed symptoms, and the inability to explain events in the first days after the strike [@hpmm-a; @hiroshima-peace-hall-a; @nagasaki-peace-hall-a]. This is the foundational matrix in which the “invisible agent” (radiation) first becomes culturally legible as a threat.

At the level of lived experience, this means a radical break between perception and understanding: the subject encounters destruction that cannot be immediately interpreted. Primary testimonies register not merely fear as an emotion, but a condition of cognitive breakdown—the inability to interpret what is happening when the body is already affected and language is not yet capable of describing it.

The further cultural dynamic unfolds from this knot: first trauma is articulated as physical destruction, then as fear of mutational futures, and later as social infection (stigmatization of hibakusha, fear of heredity, restrictions in marriage and hiring). In Nihon Hidankyo materials, this social stage is described directly and is therefore especially important for the analytical transition from bodily to social scale [@hidankyo-a; @hpmm-a].

It is here that the article’s key formula emerges: **a strike on the body produces a long-term politics of fear**, which then migrates into genre, media, and institutional scenarios of control [@dower1995a; @napier1993a].

In this context, one of the most persistent visual-ethical formulas in Japanese culture—“see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil”—is revealing, because it already presumes the limitation of perceptual channels as a form of ethical and symbolic self-regulation.[^6] Initially this formula functions as a moral injunction; from a media perspective, however, it can be read differently: as an early intuition that channels of perception themselves may become carriers of danger.

It is also important to stress that subsequent cultural dynamics do not unfold as one linear sequence. Already in the first postwar decades, several parallel regimes of trauma representation appear—from the monstrous external image to bodily disintegration and technological anxiety. These lines do not replace one another; they coexist, compete, and partially overlap.

## 3. The Monster as a Media Trace of the Strike: *Godzilla* (1954)

Here, *Godzilla* functions as a representative node within postwar imagery, not as the sole bearer of meaning.

In the research corpus, *Godzilla* is positioned as a “zero point” of postwar imagery: the monster works as a condenser of nuclear memory, making invisible radiation visible and repeatable in mass genre form [@napier1993a; @criterion-godzilla-a]. Kaiju is therefore not simply a fantastical creature, but a structural figure of historical shock.

A particularly demonstrative production principle, recorded in the recommended source, states: **“The theme of the film, from the beginning, was the terror of the Bomb.”** [@criterion-godzilla-a] In this article’s working logic, this means the following: a traumatic event is translated into an image, the image into genre form, and genre then begins to function as a medium of collective memory.

From this follows a critically important conclusion. *Godzilla* does not “replace” history with myth; on the contrary, it provides a mechanism for history’s constant return in the form of mass spectacle: the city as a repeatable scene of damage, the hospital as a site of secondary witnessing, and military/scientific discourses as a dispute over the limits of control [@napier1993a; @criterion-godzilla-a].

## 4. Bodily and Industrial Mutation: *Tetsuo* and *Mermaid in a Manhole*

For the bodily line of horror, *Tetsuo* and *Mermaid in a Manhole* serve as radicalized examples.

The second stage in the evolution of Japanese horror is a shift from the external monster to the internal body. This transition can be described in terms of bodily breakdown, decay, metalization, and the sexualization of horror.[^3] Crucially, what changes here is not only theme but the materiality of fear itself: the threat no longer arrives “from outside” in the shape of a giant creature; it grows within the subject’s own flesh.

At the same time, these forms cannot be fully reduced to “trauma work.” A significant part of extreme body horror emerges in contexts of genre exploitation, industrial pressure, and aesthetic experimentation. This means cultural memory appears here not in pure form, but mixed with commercial logic, gratuitous shock, and visual radicalization that does not always carry direct reflective depth.

In *Tetsuo*, industrial rhythm, mechanical noise, and the image of metalized flesh create a logic of violent fusion between human and technosphere. In this configuration, the body ceases to be a stable boundary of personhood and becomes an unstable interface between trauma, desire, and machine [@bfi-tetsuo-a]. This can be read as a post-nuclear-strike variant of “new flesh,” where memory of destruction works through fragmentation and overload of bodily form.

In *Mermaid in a Manhole*, the same matrix receives a different texture: emphasis shifts to rot, ulcers, disintegration, and an almost ritual contemplation of bodily ruin [@allcinema1988a]. What matters here is that horror is built not only on disgust but on the aestheticization of degradation. Cultural processing of trauma thus becomes double: it documents destruction while simultaneously turning it into an object of specific artistic organization.

The key difference between body horror and the kaiju model at this point is that the body stops functioning as a reliable border of the subject: the source of threat and the limit of the “self” coincide on one material surface.

## 5. Invisible and Networked Threat: *Kairo* as a Model of Media Contagion

*Kairo* most clearly demonstrates the shift toward a media infrastructure of fear.

By the early 2000s, the figure of threat shifts again: from bodily mutation to communication infrastructure. This transition can be described as movement toward a “networked” form of fear [@jmdb-kairo-a; @napier1993a]. In *Kairo*, the subject confronts not a physically tangible monster but a slow loss of the world’s social density: an emptying city, isolation, and “stains of absence.”

A revealing marker is the film’s promotional question: **「あなたは幽霊を見たことがありますか？」** [@jmdb-kairo-a]. It functions as an inversion of classical horror: instead of monster aggression, we get a test of the act of perception itself. Threat here spreads through a communication channel, which means contagion becomes simultaneously psychological, media-based, and urban.

If in the early postwar model fear was linked to “radiation as invisible agent,” then in the networked model this principle moves into technological environment: now “invisibility” belongs not only to matter but also to signal. This generates a new type of anxiety—not fear of death by strike, but fear of disappearance inside everyday infrastructure [@jmdb-kairo-a; @napier1993a].

In this sense, networked horror radicalizes not so much fear of death as fear of loss of presence: the subject is not directly destroyed, but “blurred,” losing density and the capacity to remain fixed in social and media space.

It is here that the bridge to article 01 is formed: the communication channel begins to work not as background but as an autonomous carrier of threat. At the transitional stage this is already visible in *Ringu*, where the recording medium becomes a mechanism of contagion, while *Kairo* extends this principle to network infrastructure.

## 6. The Sacred Dimension of Contagion: Kegare, Impurity, and Cultural Semantics of Pollution

For analytical completeness, it is necessary to include the concepts of kegare (穢れ), impurity, and “bad places,” as well as the syncretic context of Japanese religiosity.[^3] It is important to avoid reduction here: this is not a claim that modern biohorror “simply continues archaic forms,” but that in this interpretation, religious-cultural codes of pollution provide language for making sense of new forms of invisible threat [@japanknowledge-kegare-a; @hiroshima-peace-hall-a; @nagasaki-peace-hall-a].

Through this lens, contagion in Japanese horror acquires a double articulation: as physiological process and as violation of symbolic order. This is why hibakusha motifs—damaged body, “dangerous” status, and social stigma—become linked to a broader semantics of pollution. In postwar and post-terror culture, the motif of space that “must not be touched,” “must be isolated,” and “must be purified” is persistent; this concerns bodies, rooms, and social groups alike, and later directly resonates with public perception of Aum as a source of internal impurity and threat.

Analytically, this block links the article’s three levels: radiation memory of 1945, body horror of the 1980s, and network horror of the 2000s. In all cases the same structural logic operates: **dirt/contagion is not localized in one object, but spreads through relations between body, place, and community** [@japanknowledge-kegare-a; @hiroshima-peace-hall-a; @nagasaki-peace-hall-a].

## 7. Internal Techno-Apocalypse: Aum Shinrikyo and the End of the “Pure Metaphor”

The 1995 turn is radical because it transfers contagion from the zone of artistic metaphor into the zone of political-social fact. In the selected research scheme, this is the moment when evil is refigured as something emerging from within: threat no longer has to arrive as external strike; it can be produced within society itself—in a hybrid of religious cult, laboratory discipline, and apocalyptic doctrine [@psia-aum-a; @olson1999a; @opcw-aum-a].

PSIA materials, the *Emerging Infectious Diseases* publication, and OPCW materials included in the corpus indicate that this was not an isolated episode of panic, but a systemic attempt to combine ideology, chemistry, and biology in a project of mass violence [@psia-aum-a; @olson1999a; @opcw-aum-a]. Japanese cultural anxiety thereby acquires a new architecture: fear is directed not only toward destruction of the city, but toward infiltration of everyday life by hidden agents, networks, and organizations.

After this threshold, images of contagion in cinema and games are read differently. They do not merely “fantasize about the end of the world,” but reproduce the logic of internal vulnerability in late-modern society: any institution can become a threat channel, any technology a contagion carrier, and any ideology a justification for experiment on life.

Unlike earlier forms where contagion remained at the level of image or metaphor, here it is enacted for the first time as a practical technology of violence. This fundamentally changes the status of fear: it no longer requires artistic mediation, because it becomes part of empirical reality.

## 8. Late Pop Culture and Institutional Biohorror: from *Sweet Home* to *Resident Evil*

Yet this transformation is not fully continuous or linear. Between stages there are ruptures, genre reversals, and reinventions of images not always directly connected to earlier forms. This indicates that cultural memory of trauma functions not as a linear process but as an unstable field of repetitions, distortions, and reinterpretations.

The turn to games is important because fear here acquires a fully procedural form: documents, laboratory protocols, corporate infrastructure, biohazard and B.O.W. terminology, and managed mutation scenarios [@capcom-re-a; @mobygames1989a].

As a result, horror ceases to be an episode and is configured as a **risk-management system**, where threat production itself is institutionally embedded into economy and science.

Compared with earlier stages, the position of the subject also changes. If in classic kaiju cinema the human confronts an external event, and in body horror the collapse of one’s own corporeality, then in late pop-culture biohorror the subject is placed inside a bureaucratized environment where contagion is archived, tested, and replicated.

This transition is important for linking with *MGSV*: later game systems inherit not only the motif of contagion, but also the model of governing threat through classification and isolation. Fear is therefore formed not around a single monster, but around a regime of selection where bodily and linguistic differences become tools of control inside the system itself.

In this logic, later works (including the trajectory toward *MGSV*) continue an already established Japanese arc: from bodily mutation to medial and political technologies of control over life, language, and population.[^3]

## Conclusion

The extended analysis confirms the initial thesis: postwar Japanese horror is not a set of disconnected genre effects, but a historically formed and nonlinear model of cultural memory of violence. Its structure is set by recurring phases: **strike → monster → bodily disintegration → network contagion → internal techno-apocalypse → institutional bioregime** [@dower1995a; @napier1993a]. From this perspective, postwar Japanese culture moves from registering the “strike” to modeling systems in which contagion, language, and belonging become instruments of classification and control.

This is no longer only memory of catastrophe, but a way of understanding how, after historical defeats, the principles of total violence migrate into technocratic, sanitary, and media forms. In this perspective, Japanese biohorror should be understood as a social mode of trauma processing in which carriers of threat change, but the structural core remains: invisible agent, vulnerable body, infected environment, and the political question of who controls life and death. This is why analysis of the “after the nuclear strike” scenario remains productive not only for film history, but also for understanding contemporary regimes of fear [@napier1993a; @dower1995a]. At the same time, these regimes coexist and do not constitute a strictly linear evolution.

However, this analysis primarily describes the architecture of threat and only outlines the next question: how these same forms begin to function not only as objects of fear, but as stable aesthetic and cultural matrices of attraction in a broader transmedia field.[^15]
