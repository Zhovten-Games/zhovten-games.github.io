# When Disaster Becomes Environment: Chernobyl and the Spatial Model of Horror

*Preprint / Working paper / Not peer reviewed.*

## Introduction: Why “Environment” Rather than “Disaster”

In this article I use the term **“environment”** rather than neutral formulas such as “disaster” or “aftermath of the accident.” This is not a stylistic preference but a methodological position: “disaster” implies a completed event, whereas “environment” registers its ongoing presence—as risk infrastructure, as a regime of everyday life, as a form of memory [@tumarkin2005a; @nixon2011a].

Hence the central thesis of this third paper in the series: unlike the Japanese case, where trauma is localized in bodily mutation, the Ukrainian case shows how trauma **is displaced into space and infrastructure**.[^3] For contrast, see Companion working paper (Canon Horror Series, 02, 2026): *After the Nuclear Strike: The Transformation of Japanese Horror from Monster to Infected System*. In other words, the recurring formula here is: **not an event, but a regime; not a background, but an agent; not a disaster, but a risk infrastructure**.[^4]

To preserve the linear logic of the series, the material is read in three phases: **(1) latent systemic vulnerability before the accident, (2) Chernobyl as systemic rupture, (3) prolonged life inside a contaminated environment**.[^5]

\newpage

## 1. Before the Rupture: Latent Vulnerability and the Historical Memory of Environment

To understand why Chernobyl produces a specifically spatial model of horror, one must account for the fact that by the time of the accident the environment was already historically burdened.[^5]

The Ukrainian twentieth century includes experiences in which space appears not as neutral background, but as a carrier of threat and violence. The most radical example is the Holodomor of 1932–1933, where territory itself, distribution infrastructure, and the agrarian system became mechanisms of destruction [@applebaum2017a; @uinp-holodomor-a].

From this perspective, Chernobyl does not create “dangerous space” from scratch; it overlays an already existing historical configuration in which environment can function as an instrument of pressure, control, and death [@applebaum2017a; @uinp-holodomor-a].

This clarifies the article’s three-phase model: historically formed environmental vulnerability, the systemic rupture of Chernobyl, and prolonged life inside contaminated space.

The spatial character of horror here is therefore not an exception, but a continuation of a longer line in which environment repeatedly ceases to be neutral and already carries memory of violent resource distribution and control over the very possibility of survival.

\newpage

## 2. Chernobyl as Systemic Rupture, Not a Single Episode

The factual framework is established by INSAG-7, UNSCEAR, and Chernobyl Forum materials by WHO/UN [@iaea-insag1992a; @unscear2000a; @whoun2006a]. These documents register a fundamental shift: the accident is understood as a system, not an event. INSAG-7 directly shifts emphasis from the “moment of failure” to a complex of design, organizational, and governance causes [@iaea-insag1992a].

This is how the spatial logic of horror forms: threat is not exhausted by the date 1986, but reproduced in a prolonged regime of monitoring, control, and adaptation [@unscear2000a; @whoun2006a]. Therefore, the Ukrainian optic works through the formula **not event, but regime**: risk does not disappear; it is redistributed across time and territory.[^4]

\newpage

## 3. The Tragedy of Space: Not Background, but Agent

In this model, the Exclusion Zone is not décor but an active agent of memory and fear [@davies2013a; @tumarkin2005a; @nixon2011a]. This is why two concepts are especially productive for analysis: **traumascapes** and **slow violence** [@tumarkin2005a; @nixon2011a]. The first describes how place is transformed by suffering; the second shows how harm accumulates and often remains poorly visible at each particular moment [@tumarkin2005a; @nixon2011a].

Thom Davies’s formula of **“double exposure”** (radiation exposure + institutional failure) adds a political dimension to material risk [@davies2013a]. In the same article, Chernobyl as a space of **“hidden spaces of everyday life”** captures the main point: everyday life is not suspended but becomes a mode of living with threat [@davies2013a].

Hence another basic pairing in the article: **not background, but agent; not disaster, but risk infrastructure**.[^4]

\newpage

## 4. Institutions of Memory as Infrastructure of Access to Trauma

In the Ukrainian case, memory is organized not only through testimony but also through institutions: museum databases, “memory books,” and archival publications [@chornobylmuseum-a; @uinp2019a; @wilsoncenter-a]. These practices structure access to trauma: who is recorded, in what format, and with what biographical detail [@chornobylmuseum-a; @uinp2019a].

Analytically, institutions of memory—much like the game-world Zone—structure access to trauma: they define route, entry threshold, and interpretation regime for the dangerous past.[^15] Therefore, the memory block is not an “addition to history,” but part of the same regime of spatial horror.

\newpage

## 5. S.T.A.L.K.E.R.: Ecology of Threat

The S.T.A.L.K.E.R. series translates the Chernobyl matrix into interactive form, where environment acts as an autonomous force [@gsc-a; @laurila2024a].

In genre-aesthetic terms, S.T.A.L.K.E.R. is further from classic atompunk in the style of *Fallout* and closer to a post-catastrophic ecology of ruined space.

What becomes decisive is not retrofuturist atomic aesthetics, but the impact of technogenic environment, radiation risk, and anomalous space—something that may be described, provisionally, as a kind of *chornobylpunk*. The notion of the **“reactive landscape”** captures this logic precisely: the source of tension is not a single monster, but the dynamics of anomalies, emissions, artifacts, and opaque institutions [@laurila2024a].

Hence a practical ethics of route: survival is determined by how the subject reads space and manages invisible risk [@gsc-a; @laurila2024a]. This is the model of an **ecology of threat**: open environment, distributed risk, and constant behavioral adjustment.

\newpage

## 6. Metro: The Social Architecture of the Same Logic

Metro matters not as an “extra example,” but as an inversion of S.T.A.L.K.E.R. [@glukhovsky2005a; @fouragames-a; @steam-valve-a]. If in S.T.A.L.K.E.R. environment is open and unpredictable, in Metro it is closed and institutionalized: field becomes infrastructure, anomalies become social systems, distributed risk becomes stratified.[^19]

Its moral mechanics are especially revealing: the system of **“invisible moral evaluation”** or **“moral points”** operates not as external choice but as a hidden assessment protocol built into the survival system [@krottmaier2023a]. Therefore Metro does not weaken the argument; it presents its second configuration.

The short formula of this block: **if S.T.A.L.K.E.R. is an ecology of threat, Metro is its social architecture**.[^21]

Metro shows that spatial horror does not require an open Zone: it can work just as effectively in closed infrastructure, where risk is distributed across social levels, rules, and moral protocols.

\newpage

## 7. Comparative Conclusion in the Series Logic

Comparison with the Japanese paper makes the difference especially clear: if there the central question is “what does the strike do to the body,” here it is “how to live when catastrophe stops being an event and becomes environment.”[^3] If the Japanese case can be read, in broad terms, through a biopunk logic of bodily mutation, the Ukrainian case tends toward a post-catastrophic ecology, where the main carrier of threat is not organism but space as a regime of functioning. This is the transition from a bodily to a spatial model of horror.

The Ukrainian case therefore radicalizes the series’ general arc: horror is no longer localized in the figure of a monster. It is embedded in channels of existence—in route, infrastructure, access regime, and everyday adaptation to risk.[^4]

As an auxiliary comparison beyond the core corpus, comparable effects of environmental disorientation and cognitive instability are visible in other works as well [@kulikov2021a; @shyamalan2004a]. This frame is further sharpened by Alastair Reynolds’s story *Beyond the Aquila Rift* (and its screen adaptation in *Love, Death & Robots*): here the transition from hostile environment to unreliable perception advances the analysis toward the next series step—article 01, where the perception channel itself is described as threat infrastructure [@reynolds2016a].

\newpage

## Conclusion

The Chernobyl “environment” shows that cultural memory can produce horror without a central monster: a space that continuously organizes threat, survival rules, and forms of moral testing is enough to generate horror [@tumarkin2005a; @nixon2011a; @davies2013a; @laurila2024a; @krottmaier2023a]. From this perspective, the Ukrainian paper is symmetrical to the Japanese one, but fundamentally different in trauma carrier: not body after strike, but environment after rupture.[^3] At the same time, the proposed spatial model is not presented as universal and is treated as one analytical regime within a broader corpus. The next step in the series is analysis of channel as threat infrastructure (see article 01), where vulnerability is shifted from territory to media regimes of perception.
